It started as a slideshow.

That was fine. Slides work. But *fine* isn't really the point — you want people to spend time with the work, not click through it.

So the question became: what if they could walk through it instead?

---

## WHERE DO YOU PUT ART

The answer was obvious in retrospect. You put art in a museum.

**Channel 1** is a fully walkable 3D museum. WASD to move, mouse to look, `E` to read the plaques. Works hang on the walls. The lobby has a fountain. There is a gift shop.

Everything is free. Take a coffee.

---

## LEARNING THREE.JS TO BUILD IT

I didn't know Three.js going into this. I learned it for the project.

That meant a lot of reading, a lot of things breaking, and eventually going deeper into the library than the tutorials go. The PS1 rendering effect hooks into {c}onBeforeCompile{/} — Three.js's internal shader step that most people never touch. From there I'm injecting GLSL directly: vertex snapping to get that low-res jitter, Bayer dithering for the 15-bit colour look.

The whole site is TV-themed. Scanlines, CRT glow, channels you switch between. A renderer that looks like it came off a PlayStation fits that world better than a clean one would.

Sometimes the constraint *is* the design. And sometimes you learn a graphics library to pull it off.

---

## THE STAIRS

The stairs broke everything. Multiple times.

They started as straight ramps — simple, working, boring. I replaced them with curved flights wrapping around the atrium because it looked right. That decision cascaded into weeks of issues: the player snapping upward mid-climb, tiny gaps in the geometry that dropped you through the floor, the collision system not knowing what to do with a curved surface.

The fix required a proper arc-based approach. The stairs now compute curved geometry using angular math and the {c}groundAt(){/} function handles five different surface types — flat floors, upper discs, stair landings, ramps, and curved arcs — finding the right one for wherever you're standing.

Straight stairs would have been fine. They looked wrong. So I fixed it.

---

## THE DETAILS

The **floors manage themselves.** The museum reads a manifest file and builds accordingly — the right number of wings, the right number of floors. When I add enough work, a new floor opens. The "Opening Soon" signs come down on their own.

I wrote *"the museum grows as the work does"* as a throwaway line on one of those signs. I kept it.

The **gift shop** has arabic coffee. Right-click to sip. After three sips a mysterious hand reaches in from off-screen and tops it up. Shake the cup when you're done. It's a silly bit — fancy places offer free coffee, so why not — but the offscreen hand is my favourite thing in the whole channel.

The **founder's portrait** hangs on the rotunda wall. *Oil on pixels · 2026.* The plaque notes that the founder is also the security guard, the curator, and the entire gift-shop staff.

---

## WHAT IT IS

Every decision in this museum is mine. The aesthetic, the architecture, the plaque text, the gags. I built it to show my design work.

But the building is the work too.

That's probably true of any portfolio. It's just more obvious when yours is a place you can walk around in.
